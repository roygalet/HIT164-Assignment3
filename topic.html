Google Glass

Glass is an Android-powered wearable computer from Google X, a special projects division. (Reilly 2014).

Specifications

Display: 640 by 360 pixels (equivalent picture of a 25-inch HD display viewed from 8 feet away)
Camera: 5-megapixel, 720p video capture
Audio: bone conduction transducer
Storage: 16GB + Google Drive
Battery Life: one full day of typical use
Charger: micro-USB
Connectivity: Wi-Fi and Bluetooth
Variants: Prescription Spectacle, Titanium Collection, Ray-Ban, Oakley

Microinteraction

Glass augments microinteraction - providing a maximum amount of utility for the minimum amount of user attention (Starner 2013).

"Our goal is to reduce the time between intention and action" - Larry Page

So Google designed Glass with:

  • smartphone functions
  • integrate Google's services
  • gesture and voice recognition
  • ability to perform tasks instantaneously hands-free

Taking photos, just say "OK Glass, take a photo". If voice command isn't fast enough, Glass Explorers will soon be receiving a software update which allows them to take a photo by just winking (Briden 2014).

Starner (2013) also added that when the time between intention and action becomes small enough, the interface becomes an extension of the self.

Developers

Researchers at UC Berkeley have made home appliances to be controlled using Glass (Briden 2014).

A hospital in Boston has begun using Glass to scan QR codes to help them keep track of patients

In Texas, a health care entrepreneur is working with anaesthesiologists to make their rounds safer and more efficient (Della Cava 2014).

A programmer in Michigan is developing software that

  • monitor a driver's eyes
  • send visual and auditory alerts at signs of drowsiness
  • considering adding a gaming component to the app
  • would reward safe driving with points.

A firefighter in North Carolina has been programming an app that

  • routes incoming 911 calls
  • brings information to the display
  • directions
  • hydrant locations
  • building blueprints
  • car diagrams

The New York Police Department is also looking into Glass in order to see whether or not it would be a benefit to law enforcement officers working in the city (Briden 2014).

Military

According to Reilly (2014), the U.S. Air Force is beta-testing Glass for possible use on the battlefield.

Advantages:

  • low power
  • low footprint
  • sits totally above the eyes
  • doesn't hinder vision
  • access information very quickly

Possible Air Force scenarios for the technology use include

  • air traffic control
  • bombing missions
  • search and rescue
  • ground support
  • supply missions.

Gaming

Glass has so much potential for gaming on the move and Google is hoping to inspire developers with a new video demonstration (Briden 2014).

Driving

Glass users might also get

  • distracted
  • their reaction times affected
  • may lead to accidents

DriveSafe, which is free for Glass owners

  • uses built-in infrared and tilt sensors
  • detect when the head or eyelids drop
  • triggers a voice and visual alert
  • directs the wearer to the nearest rest area (Della Cava 2014).

Ethics

Arthur (2013) considers Glass as a threat to privacy as Google is a company that has repeatedly breached the boundaries of what we think is "private".

The most important question according to Arthur (2013) is - who owns and what happens to all of Glass' data.

Google has teams of people who develop algorithms

  • to locate where a device's owner is,
  • and what their past activity and searches are,
  • so Google will probably know what the owners are going to do (Arthur 2013).

Arthur (2013) also compared millions of Glass owners to CCTV cameras and Google is the monitor.

A Glass-hole refers to a person who is hooked up excessively to Glass ignoring everything around. To combat stereotyping, Google has revealed its own Top 10 Google Glass Myths (Briden 2014). This list includes reminders like,

"The Glass camera function is no different from a cell phone so behave as you would with your phone and ask permission before taking photos or videos of others."

Glass Explorer Community (2014) posted in their website,

"In places where cell phone cameras aren't allowed, the same rules will apply to Glass. If you're asked to turn your phone off, turn Glass off as well. Breaking the rules or being rude will not get businesses excited about Glass and will ruin it for other Explorers.”
